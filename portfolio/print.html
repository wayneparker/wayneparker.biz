Customer “Skyline” installation

The Challenge: Create large, permanent environmental display pieces for AT&T’s main lobby and corridors.

Might as well start out with my biggest project, in terms of sheer size: six 3′×4′ panels, overall length 24 feet.

The idea was to spotlight AT&T’s global reach and A-list customers. My creative director found nighttime photos of the skylines of six international metropolises, and wanted to overlay them with knock-out white logos of our biggest customers, along with a “swoosh” of ones and zeroes to signify our network. The swoosh was the biggest challenge, requiring quite a bit of hand blurring and layering in Photoshop to get the right look. Between a dozen effects layers for the swoosh, the high-res photos, and the sheer size, my working PSD was about 1GB!

We worked with a New York firm that specializes on building this sort of thing, and produced three: one full-size 24′ one for the lobby, and two smaller 16′ ones for the two main corridors off the lobby. As an added complication, the big one had the white logos “floating” on pieces of plexiglas mounted to standoffs, about two inches away from the surface of the display.

What we failed to anticipate is that AT&T gains and loses customers over time; so, once a year we had to update all three. The “3D” floating logos turned out to be more trouble than they were worth, so they were deleted with the first refresh.

AT&T Toggle print ad

The Challenge: Repurpose the design of a product sell sheet to create a matching full-page magazine ad.

The client wanted to use the same photo as the sell sheet to tie the two pieces together visually, but the ad needed to have more visual impact, and far less copy. I tracked down the photo, and was happy to see that there was much more that had been cropped out. However, I still had to Photoshop it to extend it and clean up some distracting elements (a brown cabinet by the sofa, a notepad on the desktop) that interfered with the copy and logo, and used it full-bleed on the final print ad.

Healthcare Security Trifold Brochure

The Challenge: Transform a boring two-page informational PDF into a polished, professional brochure.

I got this job shortly after yet another corporate branding update, this time featuring a set of pre-designed “stacked circles” as the predominant visual element. Unfortunately, there weren’t any guidelines for adapting these new elements to either strongly horizontal (website headers, for instance) or strongly vertical (trifolds, skyscraper ads, etc.), so I had to make it up as I went along, matching the pre-approved designs as closely as possible. Brand approved it, the client loved it, the sale people loved it, mission accomplished!

Event Collateral

The Challenge: Create compelling print and/or PDF collateral for a variety of corporate events.

A company the size of AT&T hosts a bewildering number of customer and sales force events each year, all requiring polished, professional-looking agendas, invitations, save-the-date flyers, feedback forms, etc. Although my responsibility was primarily front-end web development, I did a lot of these pieces for the Graphic Design team when my workload permitted. They served as a nice change of pace from debugging JavaScript or hacking around Internet Explorer bugs.

IP Flex job aid

The Challenge: Create a visual aid to make cluttered, confusing billing documents understandable to mere humans.

Don’t you love analyzing the fine print of your phone or cable bill? Yeah, I didn’t think so. But trust me, the corporate version is much, much worse.

This piece was intended to help AT&T staff assist AT&T customers to understand their quotes, contracts and bills. I used a combination of color-coding and zoomed-in focus on specific parts of the documents to clarify the important elements.
